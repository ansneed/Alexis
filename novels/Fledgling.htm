Fledgling

 

They had been eating lunch for two weeks in silence. She did not wish to ruin their strangely formed intimacy with words. She had said something to her, once, asking if she could sit next to her.

The girl was voluntarily mute. She had seen her tongue flick out from her mouth while sipping tea, thus making her very capable of speech. There was the occasional guttural sound that erupted from the base of her throat, indicating the latent ability to speak.

She had decided to give her the name Cassie. Her aunt and uncle had simply introduced the young woman as Ms. White. A fair creature with golden ringlets tied beautifully above the nape of her neck. Eyes a piercing blue. Cassandra had seemed almost too formal for this ethereal cherub, but it was her name sake. Cassie was exactly as she had imagined the doomed Trojan princess, Cassandra. One would have expected Cassandra’s reincarnation to be tan like an Arab, but her Cassandra had to be fair. She was a gifted, innocent girl, luminescent in white. Apollo had stolen this precious innocence and fated her to a life of true prophecies with no one believing her.

At least, that was the story she told herself of Cassie’s muteness.

The silence was calming to her fretted mind – it was too busy for a woman. When Cassie left to go about her mysterious day, she would watch her aunt lounge around in her vapidity.

She had been living with her aunt and uncle for three years. They had taken her and her sister in after their parents had died of pneumonia. Their aunt and uncle were much wealthier than their parents, allowing for proper introduction into society for the two girls.

Her sister had married less than a year ago. He was wealthy, scores of useless items were present in his opulent and self-indulgent mansion. He commissioned many portraits of himself in Roman garb, drawing connection between himself and Julius Caesar.

He was entirely repugnant to her; however, her sister far too easily ignored his grotesque form. His complexion was consistently rubicund, with white streaks across his face from him dragging his hands along his cheeks to wipe sweat off. This had earned him the nickname Bacon; his real name never crossed her lips.

There was no love in the union, only open desire on his end. His legs wobbled underneath his enormous girth, too skinny of ankles barely able to support him. He constantly itched his head, wig ill-fitting and a gaudy red color.

“If you want to marry rich, Catherine, you have to use your feminine wiles,” her sister would often say, primping herself up. There was an unsaid undertone to the suggestion, something she was supposed to intrinsically know as a woman. Essentially, she was expected to do everything short of spreading her legs to win a man over.

She understood what spreading her legs meant, or so she thought. No one was quite able to explain the complexities of sex more than a union between husband and wife. The books she read were unbearably vague, characters rarely kissing. Her uncle and aunt never showed more love than a chaste kiss on the cheek. It was some well-guarded secret, a manual only given once an engagement was in place.

She was in no rush to marry, even though she was sixteen, nearing the acceptable age for marriage. There was belief in love once more, not the rushed and arranged courtships of the past. Marriage for love was now, almost, becoming an accepted idea.

Her aunt and uncle were understanding for the longing for freedom, the sliver of it she had. Her days were spent leisurely, perfecting her drawing and piano playing. Books were strewn around the house in a haphazard fashion, always partially open to keep her page. Her particular favorite was by René Descartes, Discourse on the Method. Her answer to questions was increasingly “Cogito Ergo Sum;” utter nonsense, but a fanciful idea. She found it fun to say when she was dragged to a ball – she willingly went when her uncle and aunt asked her, but merely to please them, not under any volition of her own. Whoever was talking to her, whether it be a gossiping old woman or a suitor, would be taken aback and wander away, leaving her to her own devices.

Cassie had been invited over for lunch a fortnight ago. She was fraught with letters from various male suitors, an objectively gorgeous creature ripe for marrying. Perhaps, it was her silence that was so attractive: men were always praising women that refrained from speech.

She supposed Cassie was there to push her along her own tract to marriage without her uncle and aunt having to force her. She would see all the pretty things Cassie would receive and lust after them for herself.

Now, at lunch once more, she watched intently as Cassie dutifully unwrapped another parcel. Cassie was aware she had eyes on her, delicately plucking something off the folded handkerchief. She turned away to give Cassie some sense of privacy.

Cassie left soon after, giving her a small pat of comradery on her shoulder. She departed so gracefully; she doubted Cassie was aware of the whimsical movements she made.

She then returned to the list she had been keeping, cataloguing various reasons Cassie would decide to stay silent. To be mysterious was the most logical, followed by Stutter and Lacks the desire to. She held tightly onto the inkwell and stylus.

The idea had escaped her now, her mind bored. She found a book she had been leafing through before Cassie’s arrival. It was just a short collection of Greek myths. She reread the last one – the kidnapping of Persephone by Hades. She scoffed after reading it, a little tired of the abduction motif in Greek mythology, and dropped the book in favor of another.

Her day passed idly by. She read, ultimately bored by it, moving onto another task. She was busy walking around the grounds of the home before being beckoned back inside to sleep.

There was to be a ball the next day. There always seemed to be another girl being introduced into good society to be dolled up and admired by all the men in town. Little china dolls to be painted and clean for the amusement of others.

Cassie didn’t leave after lunch. She remained seated at the dining table, eyes fixated on her as she went about her usual activities. Even the sound of a piano playing caused no stir in emotion from Cassie. She was wordless.

She left to get ready for the ball, being tied into an uncomfortable and stuffy dress. It itched her legs and squeezed at her ribs. The color was not flattering either: a dark orange-red that clashed with her sun-soaked skin and dark hair.

Her acquaintance was standing at the bottom of the stairs, languidly taking in her appearance. Cassie was dressed in a navy color, highlighting her brilliant eyes and blonde hair. She doubted Cassie was uncomfortable in such tight dresses, figure perfectly angelic like the rest of her features. She was so plain next to her friend.

Her aunt waved lazily as they parted for the ball.

She had been to many balls in her life, this one was no different. Decadent almost ostentatious dresses worn, hues so brilliant her eyes began to ache. Cassie’s arm was grounding her to reality.

They greeted the hosts, a wealthy family with three unfortunately unattractive girls. All that was needed was a curtsy of gratitude. Cassie remained a silent entity, though there was no reason for her to talk. She was dignified in her gait, turning the heads of men and women alike as they descended down the stairs.

There were the usual accessories of music and young couples dancing. The married women would cluster together in various corners, drinks gripped tightly in their hands, gossiping about the unmarried women in the room.

She spotted her sister in such a cluster. Her sister gave her a warm smile, indicating her company with Cassie. Her sister’s focus was lost once more, however, to a heated discussion with the rotund woman to her left.

Cassie walked beyond the hall, past the dining room. There was no inkling of stopping. They were now on the veranda, vines twisted elaborately along the stone railing. Past the veranda were the grounds, full of flowers and trees.

She glanced at Cassie. Cassie was stoically staring ahead, small smile on her lips. She trusted Cassie. Cassie had been more truthful and open with her by not uttering a word. She never spoke a falsehood.

They continued to stroll past the veranda, the mansion behind them getting an ounce smaller with each step forward.  The light grew dimmer and the woodlands somewhat more ominous. She was struck with the thought of asking where they were going numerous times, but the words died upon her lips. Everything between them would be shattered once that line was crossed.

There was an enclave in the woods. They were maybe a mile from the house, the faintest music and illumination barring through the trees. The leaves rustled loudly in the wind, increasing her terror.

Cassie led her inside a small house in the enclave. She had almost missed it; the earthy tone it was painted blending in with the black of night. She longed to see the stars, any form of bright light.

She was surprised to find several other young people inside the shoddy structure. The floors were wooden, sloppily laid down. A small fire was in the hearth. Several rooms offshoot from the main living area.

The dim light from the fire gave everyone a ruddy glow. A red-orange hue, much dimmer than the color of her own dress. She was very conscious of how obnoxious her outfit was. Cassie patted her arm, serenely smiling and sitting down.

She deliberated about sitting on the wooden floor. She had no interest in being in the shack. Faces were clouded and dim, preventing her from reading the others’ emotions, let alone identifying them.

Three of the group – she now decided on a total number of twelve with herself and Cassie included – drew out stringed instruments, playing softly. Two violins and a viola. They had seated themselves in the corner, content at plucking their strings only furthered to suffocate the claustrophobic room.

Her eyes darted around the room, struggling to make out any distinguishable features. Cassie’s hair was nearly a source of light on its own, glowing and pure. She was able to decide four figures were male and three were female. The musicians were male as well.

Food was procured from some unknown orifice. Trifling small pastries, flaky when they touched the lips, leaving a grimy ring of white sugar. The girls next to Cassie each plucked one off the plate with eagerness. She watched as Cassie as devoured her own.

She hated being impolite. Her cheeks reddened – though she doubted anyone would be able to tell with the light from the fire – as she carefully took one for herself. She took a moment to stare at it, to see if she could unveil its contents, before deciding it was safe enough to consume.

There was an explosion of sugar and bitterness. Her features contorted from the bizarre flavoring. The other girls seemed completely unfazed, enjoying the sensations of the floral taste and odor.

The more she seemed to inhale the fumes, the lighter her head seemed to get. She swayed along with the music, entirely relaxed and without inhibitions. She was incredibly aware of a dark pair of eyes following her movements from across the room.

She felt light. The world was full of glee and goodness. She swore to see a halo hovering above Cassie’s beautifully trussed hair. Cassie had gifted her magic in the form of a sweet.

Her tongue began to feel as if it were weighed down by a bag of marbles. She tried to flick it past her lips, but she was beyond the point of feeling. Words drifted in and out of her mind, flowing freely.

A hand was on her shoulder. She glanced up to see Cassie, her image flickering along with the fire. Cassie helped pull her up, along with the owner of the dark eyes. She couldn’t really determine anything other than he was a man.

Dark Eyes and Cassie pulled her away from the living area into another to the side. It was much darker. There was a large mass in the center of the room. After careful deduction, and reassurance it wasn’t some demon manifested in the room, she decided it was a bed.

They sat her down. Cassie bent over, stroking her face and hair. The stroking was rough, almost aggressive. Her limbs were too light to be bothered though, weightless nearly.

The weightlessness continued as she noticed her dress being pulled over her head. She could breathe much better. They were just helping her adjust to her new state of being. She was laid down on the bed. Cassie kept a grip on her hand, sitting next to her on the edge.

“What should I call you?” Dark Eyes was speaking. He had taken over stroking her face, although his touch was much more delicate. She tried to will her tongue to move, to birth words, but she just ended up opening and closing her mouth. It was novel to her, how wide her jaw could suddenly open.

“I know your name is Catherine,” he teased, twirling her hair around his fingers. “But, I think I will call you Kitty. It suits you much more, my darling.” He spoke ‘darling’ as if it were an intimate nickname he had created just for her. Darling was not a pet name he used with other girls, and definitely not a word used for centuries prior.

“I have been admiring you from afar for a few weeks now. We danced once. You were graceful in your clumsiness, Kitty.” His hands skimmed along her exposed skin. “My Kitty, will you purr for me?”

He never bothered to kiss her. His hands explored areas she only touched when washing herself. But, she was a good girl, she followed her sister’s advice. He implored for more. Even in the haze, she rebuffed his more demanding requests with a shake of her head.

Cassie held her hand the whole time, eyes diverted to give some sense of privacy. It was so proper, and out-of-character, for this strange gathering.

Dark Eyes sat her up once he had his fill of pleasure. He assisted her back in her dress, lacing up the back.

“You look radiant, but green is much more your color, Kitty.” She felt him hesitate for a moment before brushing her hair aside to kiss her shoulder. Cassie pulled her up again, much more cognizant despite both having eaten the same pastry.

Cassie placed her against a wall in the living room. She swayed with the music some more, head bent, avoiding the eyes of the musicians. Cassie quickly dove into each bedroom, to check on the progress of the other couples.

She followed Cassie contently, squeezing her hand, as they walked back to the ball. The woods were much less frightening, a blurred, fuzzy edge around each tree. It all felt like some bizarre dream.

She awoke the next morning, tucked snuggly in her bed. Her hair had been brushed and it smelt as though she had been bathed. There were several angry red marks along her legs, though the source of the scrapes was unknown.

Had she dreamed the night before? Had the ball even occurred?

She sat up, head spinning and body feeling weak. There was a slight urge to retch, bile sitting patiently in the back of her throat.

And she was cold. She saw the fire blazing in her room’s hearth, but the cold was smothering her. Armed with a blanket wrapped around her shoulders, she left her room to travel to the library. She intended to read about this strange illness that plagued her now, along with the strange dreams.

But Cassie was already downstairs. Her aunt was lounging around in the library, putting away all the books she had left out. She crept into the dining room staring at Cassie.

She supposed she hated her.

Carefully, guarded, she sat down next to Cassie. All the signs were obviously present: the glazed eyes, the motions of someone using only muscle memory, the utterly mechanic and dulled behavior.

Yet, Cassie was still flawless in her beauty. The only imperfection, if one could consider it one, were the foggy eyes, seeing but not comprehending.

Cassie was unwrapping her daily pastry. She reprimanded herself for not paying more attention earlier. The floral scent wafted into the room, appetizing and promising to make the memories go away. The terror of the forest, the loss of total control, the violation of body.

Cassie noticed the longing in her friend’s eyes, smiling kindly. She shook her head to indicate the necessity of waiting. Cassie took a bite, still smiling, only causing more desire to manifest itself.

The rest of the afternoon, a time spent entirely in the company of Cassie’s watchful gaze, was deciding to acknowledge the previous night as reality. Whatever magic Cassie had passed onto her, it was unwanted and warped and yet still had given her a moment of euphoria she wished to experience once more.

At night, Cassie would help her pick out dresses and fix her hair. She assumed her aunt believed them to be going to balls. Instead, Cassie would lead her back to the terrifying cabin in the woods until her terror was transformed into a dream-like trance. Cassie would hold her hand during all encounters with Dark Eyes before dipping into the other rooms. These were the moments Cassie seemed the most alive, the depravity of the acts fueled some liveliness in those glazed eyes.

 

She soon lost track of time. Days melted into one another, becoming dichotomous periods: hours of pain and agony, shivering, and flashes of the night before, and then hours of bliss intermixed with fright and sleep.

Dark Eyes started openly courting her during the day. He would come to lunch with Cassie and would chat with her aunt animatedly. He would smirk at her, as if to communicate he did all of this out of his affection for her. She had long forgotten her earlier pleasures of reading, drawing, and playing piano. Her afternoons were essentially nightmares enacted in the light, Dark Eyes always present.

He was fond of giving her little trinkets to keep her mind ‘active.’ She had a bushel of ribbons, a few glass figurines, and several textured swatches of cloth. It was something to keep her mind busy, well rather her fingers. Her mind had become slow and unfocused, muscles reacting mechanically to daily tasks of eating and dressing.

She was huddled in a chair, stroking the cover of a book she wished she could read with her former voracity, when he sat next to her. She shuddered, twisting her body away from him.

“Kitty, darling, you have to know the purpose of my visits.” He took her hand without warning and planted a kiss on her palm. Her eyes widened, thoughts languidly forming. “I want to make you my wife. You are so deliciously quiet. I can only wonder what is going on in that pretty little head of yours. Will you do me the honor of marrying me?” Her aunt was standing by, handkerchief in hand. Cassie was collecting her belongings, satisfied with her work.

“Yes.” She spoke so softly, voice unequipped to being used. She spoke when spoken to, apparently having been modeled into the perfect wife. He was waiting, for a more enthusiastic response. “Of course,” she added, even more quietly than before. There was a pleading quality in her voice. He would take care of her; a man whose name she had never retained.

Dark Eyes kissed her for the first time at her answer, more enthused than she had expected. She caught Cassie’s eyes, noticing how the small purse she always carried swelled largely, payment complete. Cassie coldly turned to leave, never whispering a goodbye or an apology.

He had insisted on marrying as soon as possible, a suggestion neither her aunt nor uncle objected to. She found herself married within a fortnight. A grand affair she was somewhat cognizant for, her future husband having given her a much needed pastry before the ceremony. There were many more people present than she had wished. She only cared for her sister by the wedding.

Married life was a bit of a bore really. He would travel for a week or so every four months, always buying expensive and pretty presents for her. Those weeks were the worst for her. He would deprive her of those pastries so she was entirely clear-headed. By the end, she would be able to read some books and remember how to play a song or two, but the need to return to the pain-free high was too strong. She would fly into his arms when he returned, covering his face in kisses, indulging in every one of his pleasures.

However, during her weeks of awareness, she would find herself attending events with her sister in the evenings. To experience balls not only as a married woman, but also sentient, made her privy to Cassie’s movements.

She quite resented Cassie. The descendent into dependence to Dark Eyes and the pastries were linked to her. Cassie walked around with dignity, still beautiful, and still inexplicably single.

Cassie, the harbinger of magic, the stealer of innocence, continued to seduce young women with her silent charms. A siren of a different nature, having spellbound the youth until they too were too deeply submerged in the ocean for their innocence to survive. Her innocence had died the day she had decided to befriend Cassie, if she had any will to friend her at all.

The girls were always like her, before Cassie. Girls that were prettily dressed but entirely preoccupied with their independence and not suitors. Things meant nothing to them, men inconsequential.

The time it took to break the girls depended on how strong-willed they were. None were ever strong enough to resist the allure of Cassie’s world.

 Her hatred of Cassie was never enough to convince her to intervene; love was a farce novels and plays sold to gullible women. Lies of freedom and passion, feelings that never came to fruition.

She studied her fellow fledglings wordlessly. Fledglings never quite ready to fly, to leave the nest, but had the urge to. The fox at the foot of the tree, urging the fledglings that they were ready. “Fly, little birdies, fly,” the fox said as the fledglings took the monumental steps out of the nest and into her gullet.
