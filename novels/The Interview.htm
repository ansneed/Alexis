The Interview

 

People always stared at her when she laughed. It was a monumental affair: head thrown back carelessly, hair rippling in some magically conjured breeze, with a laugh that filled all empty space.

She was laughing now, cigarette precariously balanced between two fingers. The smoke wafted lazily into the café air, mixing with the aromas of espressos and teas.

“Can I interview you?” She cocked her head back, absorbing the image of her questioner.

“Why?” Her words were drawn out, dramatic. She enunciated clearly.

“I find it funny that you’re smoking, hardly anyone does anymore.” She smiled, taking a drag of her cigarette for show.

“Is this for a class? You’re definitely not a reporter.” A shrug was the answer. Class it was then. She indicated the seat across the table from her.

“Is that a yes?”

“I asked you to sit, didn’t I?” She arched an eyebrow, blowing some smoke from her nostrils, an effect not quite as sexy as she would have imagined. “Why is my smoking so transfixing? You’ve obviously never been to the American South.”

“I don’t think we would be very welcome there.”

“Oh, you’re quite mistaken; they’re quite welcoming.” Another drag from her cigarette. “Do you want one?” She waved the carton in front of her interviewer.

“I don’t smoke…cigarettes at least.” A sly smile tickled her lips.

“Do you have a name I should call you?”

“Blake.”

“That’s a very gender-neutral name. Did your parents name you before they knew your gender?” Another shrug. “Right, right, I’m the one being interviewed.”

“It was your nails.” She waited for more, an elaboration. “You’re not like other smokers. You’re younger for one and your teeth aren’t too stained from the tobacco yet. But, but, it was your hands. Your nails are immaculate. At first, I was trying to decide if you had simply painted them today, but it seemed unlikely. Maybe you get them redone once a week. There’s no chipping though, so I settled on fake. Glamorous, fake red nails with no marks of ash.” She took a moment to admire her nails, fake they were. They clacked on the table after she snuffed her cigarette.

“Very observant.” Her fingers ran through her hair, disengaging the tangles. She shook her hair around afterwards for good measure. “So, what do you want to know?” Her legs were crossed, revealing an equally as red pair of pumps.

“That’s not the kind of interview I need. Just, talk to me.” Her interviewer said as vaguely as possible.

“Well, I need a drink then.” She called over for the waiter. “Are – are you old enough to drink?”

“It’s eleven in the morning.”

“On a Friday. It’s acceptable to drink this early on Fridays.” She smiled flirtatiously at the waiter, taking enjoyment out of his lusty looks. She ordered two fairly complicated drinks for both of them.

“Sorry, I’m a bit flustered is all. This will calm me down.” She explained as the waiter set down the drinks. Another toothy smile graced her lips, perfectly poised to render the waiter helpless. His mouth jerked into a quick smile, and he stumbled over his feet as he walked away backwards. Already, she had another cigarette lit, ash tumbling onto the table like a light snowfall.

“You don’t seem flustered.”

“Not outwardly, no.” She puffed into the cigarette, daring it to produce more smoke. “You don’t find this strange, either?” A shake of the head. No. “You’ve done this a lot? Going up to strangers to write down their stories? A biographer of the real people?” There was a definite hint of sarcasm, perhaps more of a gentle teasing.

“That’s an oversimplification of what I do.” She threw her head back to laugh, soaking in the attention. The ash and smoke from the cigarette framed her eerily, evoking an almost deathlike quality to her. She was an icon of all that was disappearing from the world.

“You are the fascinating one, Blake.” She leaned over, resting her chin on the back of her hand. “Now, am I supposed to delve into a backstory of how I came from a poor family of coalminers and longed to escape from the shadows of the Appalachia only to find myself at a ‘French’ café that serves no French food?”

“Only tell it to me if you can make it believable.” She chuckled this time, much more contained and seductively.

“No, my story is much more common. I grew up middle class, not really bothering with the fact I was different from most of the other little girls. I went to a state college, majored in business, and work as a consultant.”

“No you didn’t.” She was only momentarily taken aback by the comment. Her drink and cigarette were in the same hand. She was alternating between the two, achieving the faintest of buzzes. “I mean, I believe everything until the business major. You’re a theater major, an actress.”

“What makes you say that? Can’t a consultant dress well and have fun?”

“Yes, a consultant can. All I’m saying is you’re an actress. I told you to make me believe.”

“I thought this was for an interview. Aren’t interviews used to learn the truth about someone?”

“Not anymore really.” She sipped at her drink, stomping out her second cigarette. She took a moment to study her interviewer again.

“You’re lying to me, too.” The interviewer shrugged. “It appears as if we are at an impasse. How am I to trust anything you say and you I?”

“Fuck if I know.” She dared to laugh again, halting all other conversations. A few other snickered along, not even aware of what they were laughing at.

“You haven’t even asked my name.”

“All for the purpose of you remaining anonymous.” She was staring up at the parasol shading the table. A spider was in the process of finishing its web.

“Okay, how about you tell me a fact about yourself, and I’ll follow?”

“You’re my subject.” The interviewer protested.

“Yes, but I have already revealed some aspects about my life earlier, whether or not you choose to believe them.”

“Alright. I, too, grew up in a middle class family as an only child, attended a state school, and majored in history.” She uncrossed her legs, resting her elbows on the table.

“I like to read Russian literature in my free time.”

“Tolstoy?” She nodded as an answer. Her drink was nearly empty, condensation droplets racing to the bottom of the glass. She traced circles in the table with the loose water.

“You’re finally telling the truth.” Her interviewer’s drink was empty now. She had watched as the contents had been emptied and swallowed.

“No, I’m only making it believable.” She was subdued now. A lighter was procured and a third cigarette lit. She thanked her interviewer quietly.

“I have to go now. It was really pleasant, our conversation.” Her hand was patted, bills scattered on the table. “Come back Monday, same time.” Her eyes stung from the smoke, or at least that was the lie she was telling herself.

 

She listened to her interviewer, returning back on Monday. A cigarette butt was already in the ash tray, not having been cleared out by the wait staff yet. The same waiter from Friday approached her, kindly bestowing compliments. She laughed when she was supposed to, adoring the glances of love and jealousy. That strange mix of adoration one felt while in the audience of a spectacular show, filled with people much more attractive and talented.

He handed her an envelope after their brief flirtation. It was harmless really. He was under the impression she held the world at her fingertips. There was pride in having been chosen as a passing game.

To the Smoking Lady was written on the outside in a messy scrawl. She used her nails to peel the glue from keeping the envelope closed. A letter slipped out unceremoniously, growing slightly damp from all the residue of the unclean table. The contents were typed.

I was told to find a woman to cast to be my co-star. You requested someone with experience. You meant acting experience, no doubt, not experience in life’s miseries. I found the woman, scrounging around cafes as I often do. Actresses like to hang out around cafes, usually as a waitress or hostess. This woman was different, entirely debonair, oblivious she is in an era that rejects her. She smokes like a chimney, drinks before noon, and has an air full of confidence and insecurities. She craves attention like no other, faking a laugh, her story. I don’t know her name, but she’ll come. When you see her, you’ll love her, too. I want to recommend her as my top choice for the new Blanche in our remake.

Below were more words in the scrawl.

Casting auditions start on Tuesday at 8:00 A.M. at Studio 34. Show them the letter and they’ll let you in. – Blake

People had laughed at her in college, she never told Blake that. They laughed at her dream of becoming an actress, for finding resonance with Anna Karenina. She went about it wrong, sitting in cafes instead of waiting tables to make money.

Tears welled in her eyes again, no smoke to blame this time. Instead, she fixed a smile on her face, ordering a glass of champagne as was expected. The words, blatantly describing her insecurities were ignored: she had made it.
