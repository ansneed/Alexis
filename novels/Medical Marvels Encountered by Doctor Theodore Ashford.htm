Medical Marvels Encountered by Doctor Theodore Ashford (pages 57-66)

 

I have found evidence of the divine – no doubt this will be discounted by science in the future. Ancient storm gods like Baal and Neptune predict this. Yet, divine it was. I write this but a mere after I met with this peculiar patient, this unique specimen with no traces of physical aging.

I had been working early this morning, making my rounds to see homebound patients, when Mrs. Gallows, a rather rotund woman with a serious bout of gout (I had no intention of rhyming), pulled me aside.

“Dr. Ashford, I have heard of a most strange woman.” She told me in her attempt to whisper – it was rather loud, enough to startle her cat lurking on the bedroom dresser.

“Is she in need of medical assistance?” I inquired, usually not privy to village gossip. Mrs. Gallows has a terrible habit, an addition, almost a malady, of being a rumor mill. You see, you have to play along for the good of the patient. Being a gossiping old woman is not classified as a disease – yet.

“Oh, not that I’m aware. I was in the local bazaar the other day, before my foot became infected with rot, and was approached by a girl from my youth. However, she has not aged past her prime! Quite peculiar, innit?” This snippet succeeded in catching my attention, the bee to the flower’s nectar. Mrs. Gallows peppers her speech with foreign words in an attempt to sound educated, often lapsing to an accent and speech pattern more in line with her working class upbringing when she gossiped. So, often.

Mrs. Gallows – alright, I shall admit I divulge my side pursuit of tracking unusual medical afflictions to several companions – knows of my side research.

“Hmm…that is quite peculiar.” I said in agreement. I packed up my medical equipment into my leather bag, my sturdy companion. “What is this woman’s name? Her address? I wish to call upon her to verify your claim.” I stood up abruptly, bumping my knee into Mrs. Gallows’ nightstand – she must’ve moved it since my visit yesterday. I will not take the pains to describe Mrs. Gallows’ bed chambers again – to inquire a clearer picture of the environment, flip back to pg. 42.

“She currently goes by the name Tallulah Jordan, but I knew her as Henrietta Lyle. Ms. Jordan, Lyle – I’m not really sure what to call her – lives on Carter Hill Ln, by the Smither’s shoe shop. Her house is the first on the left.” As Mrs. Gallows continued to trip over her words, doubt crept in my mind. Mrs. Gallows is not a reputable source in any sense of the word, but I’ll admit, my curiosity piqued. My drive to discover the unknown shielded itself against pesky logic and rationality – I refuse to miss another opportunity to astutely observe another unique affliction.

Anyways, I thanked Mrs. Gallows for her information and bid her good day. After leaving her company, I followed the directions kindly dictated to me by Mrs. Gallows and found myself standing in front of the supposed Ms. Lyle’s house. A conspicuous piece of architecture construed of beige siding and a boxy, two-floor structure.

My heartbeat quickened as my breath became utterly tangled in my throat. Oh how excited I get from the anticipation – to stand at the cusp of discovery. I’d like to think, to fancy myself, like an explorer of old, Leif Erikson or Marco Polo, trekking across unrecorded and mysterious terrain to only set my eyes on something new to myself, inexplicably beautiful.

The creature that answered the door to my rapt knocking was bewitching. I could easily see how the likes of lesser men would be speechless in her presence, a siren-incarnate.

“Good morning, Ms. Jordan; my name is Dr. Theodore Ashford. This may sound odd, preposterous even, but a patient of mine suggested she knew you from her youth, nearly fifty years ago.” Ms. Jordan sighed.

“Why don’t you come inside, Dr. Ashford?” She said, stepping aside. I crossed the threshold brusquely, bowing my head in gratitude.

I shall now pause the narrative to describe Ms. Jordan and her quarters. Mr. Jordan stands at average height for a woman, 5’4”, with a slender build. She has an oval face, aquiline nose, light brown eyes, and long black, plaited hair. She is what some men could describe as ‘curvy’ being well-endowed. The interior itself was covered in dust, as if abandoned for many years. A lone rug decorated a living room; a small armchair with a floral pattern in its corner. An opened book lay on the chair – to which I say upset me more than the dust; the thought of a book’s binding weakening and becoming desolate unnerved me. The floors were all wooden, tainted from over-exposure to sunlight, and the paisley wall paper having faded and starting to peel in the corners. Past the chair was a small kitchen and a wooden dining table with chairs. It is there she directed me.

“Would you like some water?” She asked, pouring two glasses of water for us automatically.

“Thank you Ms. Jordan.” I said as she placed the glass in front of me, making no offer of a coaster to protect the table.

“What you heard is a lie.” She said.

“You aren’t even aware of what I was told, Ms. Jordan; that is quite the assumption.” She shrugged in response to my reply. “If I may say, Ms. Jordan, there are only two paths that could’ve led us to the present situation: you were born Ms. Henrietta Lyle and somehow reached a point in your life where you stopped aging or you are Ms. Tallulah Jordan who happens to strongly resemble Ms. Lyle.” The grin spread widely on Ms. Jordan’s face, a sign she believed she had outwitted me – however, this also makes people spill the beans if you will, to gloat in her victory.

“I told you, you was wrong.” Ms. Jordan said, crossing her arms and returning to a dialect she no longer spoke. She must’ve been raised a working class girl, having lost the finer intricacies of her dialect with schooling – a fact I corroborated with her later. As aforementioned, Mrs. Gallows follows similar speech patterns while excited or flustered.

“Please enlighten me, Ms. Jordan.”

“You won’t call me crazy?” She asked, leaning back in her chair.

“Ma’am, I am a rapt listener. Mysterious maladies are my bread and butter.”

“I was born Henrietta Lyle nearly sixty years ago now. I grew up a few houses down, living a normal life like ev’ryone else.”

“Do you have your birth certificate Ms. Lyle? Pictures perhaps? All the authentication you have to prove this is the case.” The woman who I will now refer to as Ms. Lyle rose from her chair to another adjoining room. She returned a minute later with a shabby shoe box, tattered remains of a brand name and eaten away corners. She lifted the lid and handed me her birth certificate and a yellowing photograph with a date nearly forty years earlier scribbled on. “Please continue.” I said, examining both artifacts closely, trying to guess their age. No wool would be pulled over my eyes, not again after the March 21st incident.

“When I was 20, I met a boy. I would’ve called him a man, but after being ‘round long as I have, boy’s the right word. He was one of those rich-boy, bad-boy types, always gettin’ in trouble because he was bored and he could afford to.” Ms. Lyle procured a picture before I even asked her to. She is standing, looking very much the same as she does now, next to a young, haughty boy. His stubble is growing out, light hair longer than the usual fashion worn today, and with strong facial features indicating a good and nourished upbringing. The picture has been taken atop of foothill, suggesting guarded recklessness.

“Womanizer? Charming?” I asked for clarification, taking note how the photo dated prior to the other had had shared with me.

“Charming, yes, but not a womanizer, at least not with us poorer girls. I think he did it to piss his mom off, dating me. Anyways, we both unexpectedly fell in love, quite suddenly, quicker than the snap of your fingers. We made love after two weeks of knowin’ each other and when lying in bed together, he cursed me.” She paused for dramatic effect, having practiced the tale – a rehearsal of her life’s monologue.

“In what way, Ms. Lyle?”

“He wished for me to be as beautiful as I was in that moment, for the rest o’ my life.”

“A wish hardly precludes a change in basic anatomical physiology.” Doubt returned unceremoniously, a thief carrying his treasure into the night.

“I told you not to call me crazy.” Ms. Lyle snapped. I plopped my medical bag on the table, pulling out my various instruments that would need to be used.

“I’m going to ask, if it isn’t too intrusive, to conduct several tests on you, such as blood pressure, heart rate, vision, hearing…” I trailed off, placing the stethoscope around my neck.

“Go ahead. You’ll be just as perplexed as all the other doctors.” Fortunately for me – not so much for Ms. Lyle – she had been correct in her statement. Her physical appearance most definitely did not match the apparent aging of her body and organs.

From the little I could do given the circumstances, I found Ms. Lyle had an astronomically high blood pressure, weak vision, poor hears, and lungs of a lifetime smoker (as a note, I would like to assure all that I washed my hands thoroughly before completing her check-up due to the lack of gloves I had on my person). If I had been able to complete blood work or even test a sample of her urine, I’d be sure to find a high cholesterol.

The worst, by far, were her reflexes, knees so worn with several probable tears to both her ACL and meniscus, barely could unbend, protesting the movement with their entirety.

“Do you exercise Ms. Lyle?” I asked after having taken careful note of all her ailments.

“Yea, I walk a few miles every day. It’s all my body can take anymore.” I had a suspicion she was lying, namely due to her blood pressure, but I digress.

“Ms. Lyle, may I make some recommendations?” She threw up a hand, stopping me.

“No, no. I don’t wish to prolong my natural life.” She folded her hands.

“Okay Ms. Lyle. Would you indulge me in continuing your recollection?” She flipped her hair over her shoulder, body appearing so finely sculpted and toned it made it almost impossible to believe my own findings. I still doubted some of the veracity of her story, of how she came to be.

“We thought nothing of it. I still felt like I was aging, just not outwardly. He proposed a few months later and we married.

“It wasn’t until I was almost 30 we noticed the problem. We couldn’t get pregnant. How could I stay beautiful while pregnant? I had so many miscarriages, almost bleedin’ out twice. Our marriage became increasingly strained, both entirely unsure of the root of my condition. He kept agin’, I didn’t.

“We started gettin’ looks. The town started talkin’, so we moved ‘way for a few years. On acceptin’ we never have children, our lives became happier.

“We returned 10 years ago, many of our peers havin’ left the town for the countryside. It felt safe, but it wasn’t. The looks returned, but from hungry yung men. He could only do so much to fend them off, but we were both so old…” Ms. Lyle trailed off, leaving the rest for me to construct myself.

“Where is your husband?” I asked the obvious question. She sighed loudly.

“He passed while trying to protect me.” The book in the other room served probably as memoriam for her deceased husband, a talisman if you will.

“So you changed your name and moved here?” She nodded, sipping her glass of water. “Are you still harassed?”

“Not recently, at least not physically. I still looks from dem boys, not dissimilar from you, but I only go out during the day for food and often with a friend.” I brushed aside the offense at Ms. Lyle’s aside, the words from an obviously bitter woman. To be young is to be ogled at, but most reactions are of a primal instinct out of our control. As a doctor, it is my profession to ignore the primal instincts and act in a way that ladies feel safe, unthreatened.

“I apologize if my behavior was anything less than professional.” Ms. Lyle shook her head.

“No it’s fine. You look at me like a person rather than an object.” I nodded, replacing my equipment back in my bag.

“Ms. Lyle, I would like to ask several clarifying questions.” She stood up to pour herself more water, sipping steadily before sitting back down.

“I don’t know how this happened.” She said, eyes darting away.

“I believe you have some inkling, at least more than the explanation of a wish. Why would you be chosen out of everyone who vainly desires youthful looks?” She shrugged and pulled out a pack of cigarettes from her purse askew on the chair beside her. “Ms. Lyle, smoking is detrimental to your health; I advise against it.” Ms. Lyle rolled her eyes childishly, lighting up at the table.

While she stared out the kitchen window – a rather small sparrow had settled on its ledge, whistling piercing inside – I pulled the shoe box closer to me. It was like a child’s version of a safe.

There were two stacks of photos, all meticulously dated with the photo’s location, that I perused through. Most pictures were of Ms. Lyle and her husband, him growing older – albeit slowly – with each photo. The lack of childhood photos of Ms. Lyle struck me as odd.

“Are you certain you’re not a succubus?”

“What that?” Ms. Lyle drew from her cigarette, question masked by the cloud of smoke. I silently crossed off the succubus explanation. I have found that turning to mythology often has aided me with finding a name or cause to the strange ailments I have encountered. However, Ms. Lyle does not follow the same patterns as the three female succubae I have come in contact with; each having a viral infection altering their brain chemistry to crave consumption of male genitals and retain it as energy and youth – often producing children through seemingly immaculate circumstance, the ingestion of semen producing offspring.

Honestly, the best comparison I have is a reversed Tithonos – Aurora (known as Eos in the Greek myth) situation: instead of eternal life but not youth, Ms. Lyle has been granted the opposite.

I continued to rummage through the box, finding several love letters between the spouses, until I came across a ratty letter at the bottom. Ms. Lyle tried to prevent me from reading it, having realized her cockiness may have been her downfall. Hubris at its finest.

The letter had been written from Ms. Lyle’s husband, who’s name I shall not reveal at this current time, and his mother. To summarize the letter best: their familial line had been blessed with extended lives, often spanning several centuries, and he has passed some of this gift onto Ms. Lyle upon making love. From what I could determine – please note, this is a rough hypothesis I have formed to be tested thoroughly in the future – they have a small, reclusive community where they only breed with others with the same affliction, but by having intercourse with someone outside their strange genetic anomaly resulted in Ms. Lyle’s state. Or, perhaps it wasn’t until the verbal consent did her future husband pass the gift onto herself. Without her notice, I took a blond strand of hair, from a rather large bundle, which I presumed to be her husband’s hair and stashed it in a pocket in my bag.

“Ms. Lyle, why did you withhold this from me?” I handed the letter to her after I had read its contents.

“I didn’t. I told ya it was a curse.” She snapped, snatching the parchment so crumpled and crinkled in her hand. I flinched from the evidence being handled so haphazardly.

“May I take some cultures to study further in my lab? Blood samples, skin grafts, swabs, to test if there is a genetic or biological anomaly present within you.”

“I think we’ve talked enough.” She stood up abruptly, marching back to the front door. “Get out.” She said, flinging the door open and giving me little choice but to leave. As I walked past her, with my belonging, I managed to pluck a loose strand of hair off her shoulder. I have never held anything tighter than that strand. There were many unanswered questions, ones that I wished Ms. Lyle had been keen to answer.

Upon returning to my abode, I placed her hair in a vile, along with her husband’s, and returned to my remaining duties. At one, I visited Mr. Rudolph with two broken ankles and a rather nasty bout of influenza and at three I tended to young Timothy Crux with a mild case of chicken pox. Once prescribing the medication and the course of treatment needed, I found myself with a small window of time before Mrs. Willoughby prepared dinner for the other tenants and myself.

The only problem I face is not knowing how old the husband’s specimen is – whether it be before or after he passed along the ‘curse.’ I took measures to examine my own hair as a control, rather confident in my ordinariness.

As it often is when studying hair, there is little to determine from appearance alone. Each strand, although different colors and textures, appeared the same; although, only my hair had split edges and signs of weathering.

Then, I decided to do something a man of science should never do when facing a limited supply of samples: attempt to destroy it. I could do what I please to my own hair and began with doing tests on various strands, five total.

I burned my hair – burnt hair always leaves the ghastliest odor; I had to open a window so I could continue my work. I soaked my hair in bleach, causing it to transform into a light-blond color and become rather course. Next, I cut up a strand of hair using a knife. For my fourth method, I pulled at the strand to create stress until it broke. The last rudimentary test I completed did not have the objective of destroying my hair strand, just to test a reaction. I soaked my strand in alcohol for five minutes to find the hair coated in a somewhat sticky coating and damage done to the ends after dried.

After recording the reactions to my own hair, I tested my other two samples from gentlest to least. The husband’s hair responded to some of the harsh treatments, but didn’t break until I set it on fire, scissors and pulling rather useless. The damages were visible, but the tests were much less effective.

The true marvel was Ms. Lyle’s hair. No outward damage could be seen – no split ends and a resistance to being cut by the scissors. To my astonishment, the hair did not set on fire either, the flame becoming extinguished upon touching the strand – on multiple occasions.

Unfortunately, I was called to dinner and halted my progress to eat a rather bland bowl of chicken broth and stale bread. Once I returned to my quarters, I placed what remained of her husband’s in the microscope and then her own. Ms. Lyle’s hair, upon further inspection, demonstrated all the signs consistent to the trials I subjugated it too – on the inside of the follicle. The outward appearance was completely unharmed with only the inside serving as a marker to the abuse it had sustained. Her husband’s hair told a different story with the inside of the follicle appearing in much better condition than hers.

It appears, despite his romantic (I would say selfish really, subjugating a poor, young, impressionable girl to elongated beauty without her consent to satisfy his own carnal pleasures, though he probably was unaware of the implications of his actions) motives, Ms. Lyle’s husband had retained some of his former qualities of advanced life while hers only affected the surface.

More testing needs to be completed upon learning of this anomaly – I will resolve myself to search for Ms. Lyle’s mother-in-law to obtain a better picture of the situation and perhaps determine the physical and genetic differences between myself and her.

Ms. Lyle, although an interesting case study, seems to be an unwilling participant and only the beginning of my newest discovery.

Note to self: please provide Mrs. Gallows with a thank of you sort, maybe flowers.

Tomorrow’s schedule: 11 AM – Ms. O’Donnell; 12 PM – Mr. and Mrs. Carver; 3 PM – young Celeste Campiglio.

I have decided to title Ms. Lyle’s condition novis aurora for the reverse of Dawn’s fatal mistake of asking for eternal life for her lover but not youth. I wonder how often the rash decisions of youth have resulted in cases similar to Ms. Lyle.

For now, without seeing any clear biological or genetic components, I shall leave this case marked as unresolved – the result of some power I am not aware of, but I am determined to solve this perplexing riddle myself. I shall slay the clever sphinx, besting foolish talk of magic with medicine and logic again.

 

Until tomorrow, Dr. T.M.A.
