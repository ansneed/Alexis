The Last Immigrant

Her mother had not spoken the language when they immigrated – she hadn’t either. The only difference between their respective lingual learning curves was age. By the time she had reached the same age as her mother when they had immigrated, she spoke flawlessly and her mother in sparsely related fragments.

She didn’t consider her childhood happy, teased mercilessly about her lingering accent and wobbly legs. The other children had called her “Heavy” and “Fatass”, “Dirty” and “Mouthbreather.” She had fought hard against those stereotypes, to educate those having lived a sheltered life. The names had eventually subsided, but the treatment never did. She had been the only one, the only immigrant of her kind, the last one.

Maybe – at least she attributed her current situation to her childhood traumas – that was the reason why she had chosen teaching as her profession. The last immigrant, a teacher of History no less. She taught the next generation of its roots, the pride they should have. Her only issue: she couldn’t remember. She knew enough to get by – sometimes unsure if the few memories were her own or creations from her extensive reading – and could answer questions for her students, but her own children were much less impressed.

Finally, after months of obnoxious begging – and because her youngest, Celeste, needed more in-depth answers for a school report – she agreed to make the trip to her mother’s.

 

“Mom, do you have to come?” Celeste said, irritation evident in her tone. Her arms were crossed, sulkily focusing on a nearby row of bushes.

“You won’t be able to understand Grandma otherwise.” She said, inadvertently slowing her steps.

“Yeah, but you’ll change her answers.” Celeste rolled her eyes as she spoke, suggesting her statement was nothing but face.

“I promise I won’t.” Celeste didn’t seem to hear her mother. She swore she heard Celeste mutter, “You don’t even like her.” Her own insecurities and qualms with her mother always seemed to resurface as Celeste and her older sister, Valerie, went through periods of curiosity. She never faulted her mother for leaving, only for not doing it sooner. She resented the fact her mother had stubbornly never learned the language, that she thought the opportunity of a better life had been squandered.

Such emotions had led her to relocating her mother into a nursing home rather than allowing her to sit in her home and judge. Not that judgment would’ve been effective – she barely understood their native tongue anymore!

The walk to the nursing home, Tranquil Living, only took thirty minutes. As they approached the gate, she noticed excitement dancing across Celeste’s face, her handwritten notes being crinkled by increasingly tightening fists. Once inside, she signed their names and allowed Celeste to lead the way.

“Grandma!” Celeste shouted, running into the small single, throwing her arms around her huddled grandmother.

“Darling.” She croaked, straightening her back and embracing her. “Stephanie.” Her mother acknowledged her finally. Celeste sat on her grandmother’s bed after drawing open the stiffening curtains with a loud snap. Stephanie crinkled her nose at the aroma of mildew, hastily having been covered in cleaning products. Celeste folded her legs underneath her, not moving as her mother sat beside her. Stephanie’s mother sat in an armchair by the small table considered a desk.

“Why you come?” Her mother asked, folding her hands in her lap, struggling to speak in her forcibly learned tongue.

“I have to do an interview with a grandparent for school.” Celeste said, waiting patiently as Stephanie repeated her words.

“Okay.” Celeste beamed.

“Okay Grandma, why don’t we start off easy, why’d you immigrate?” Celeste asked. Stephanie paused before translating the question, wanting to scold Celeste for her naivety.

“The water kept rising, devouring our land. We didn’t really have a choice – the government started tearing us away from our homes and to ship us here.” Her mother answered, no trace of gratitude in her voice. Stephanie could only compare her mother to an oblivious, obstinate animal, stranded in a dying, ravaged land too stupid to realize her fate.

“Grandma said it was because of the flooding. The water kept creeping up, making us build out homes taller until the conditions were so bad we had to leave.” Stephanie said, careful to not repeat the reasons she had provided to her children over the years, watching as Celeste’s hand sprinted across her notes.

“What exactly happened to Grandpa?” Celeste asked, pen tapping the page.

“She wants to talk about Dad.” Stephanie relayed quietly to her mother. Her mother dabbed her eyes, glaring away.

“Tell her he died in that house, doing the work of the government and we couldn’t bring his remains. They made me leave my husband.” Stephanie sighed, putting her mother’s words in plain terms.

 

She couldn’t remember her father – he had died while she had been a baby – he had weak lungs, but had insisted on finishing his conscription and staying with his land and wife.

She did remember leaving though: her mother digging her nails into the water-damaged floorboards, begging to be left in her home. The men in hazmat suits scooping them both up, unable to communicate, to take them to their new home. The benevolent visitors, similar looking but ultimately superior for having known when to leave.

 

“What’s the biggest difference between your home and here?” Celeste – and then Stephanie – asked.

“They’re lazy, spoiled, and ungrateful. They don’t know how to do anything with their hands.”

“Mom, she’s twelve.” She almost added that she was too arrogant to bother learning the language.

“Tell her.” Her mother demanded.

“The reliance on technology rather than human power.” Stephanie eventually said. Celeste didn’t question her mother, seemingly having forgotten all earlier reservations of lying.

 

A few months prior, Stephanie had taken Valerie to visit, again for a school project. Her mother always spoke of her marvelous cooking skills, in that she could still make food from scratch. The nursing home had been kind enough to scrounge up old rations to let them try and make cookies. Stephanie had been skeptical, her mother not really being able to read, but allowed Valerie and her to try. Valerie and her mother had been drawing in her room to wait for the oven to finish baking when Stephanie noticed the cookies were little more than unmixed white blobs. She had quickly replaced them with premade cookies, heating them up in the oven. Her mother still bragged of her victory, never knowing that Stephanie had trashed her ineptitude. A fitting metaphor for their life together.

“What did the air taste like? The water feel like? Was it different than here?” Stephanie translated for her mother, noting how her forehead creased.

“Why does she ask me these things? Didn’t you tell her anything?”

“I don’t remember anything.” Stephanie said.

“What’s going on?” Celeste asked, turning to face Stephanie. The evenness of her stare, the underlying accusation, caused Stephanie to stir.

“I confused her with my wording.” Her mother eyed her warily before giving a brief response. “The air tastes the same here, but it is less heavy and never too hot or cold. Water is clearer, but it feels wrong. The weight from it is gone.” Stephanie grew tired, out of practice from code switching. Celeste took some more notes, starting at her grandmother with intrigue. Intrigue from studying a relic of the past, preserved behind some invisible museum glass, not a living human.

“What about real gravity?” Celeste asked, curiosity obvious as she leaned over with her arms resting on her knees.

“No different.” Her mother said without her translator.

“Has anyone asked you about this before?” Stephanie inadvertently shook her head with her mother. “But why?” Celeste whined.

“They had enough accounts from earlier immigrants.” Stephanie spoke softly.

“But you were the last.” Celeste continued to persist, injustice causing her eyes to tear up. Celeste had always been the sensitive child, taking time to learn about her foreign grandmother, adopting a baby bird without permission, giving her prized stuffed owl away to their neighbor.

“They had enough studies done that interviews weren’t really seen as helpful anymore.” Stephanie said, arms outspread to embrace her daughter. Celeste took a few moments to sigh loudly and fight the tears. Stephanie and her mother exchanged glances; the memory of being interrogated at the customs office.

 

Fear marked Stephanie’s past, no words and grainy images to accompany the feeling. She did recall the Census Taker, every two years, asking increasingly humiliating questions about their health, their intelligence, their social status.

 

“Do you think we could ever go back?” Celeste asked after calming herself down. Stephanie resisted shaking her head again.

“Celeste, do you want to finish the rest of your written questions?” Celeste glared at Stephanie, moving away from her on the bed. More dust erupted into the air, smoldering the air and its surroundings like volcanic ash. Her throat closed at the mere thought.

“How do you think it happened?” Celeste waited impatiently as Stephanie reformed the question. Her mother shrugged as an answer. “Why did you wait so long to leave?” Her mother grew agitated as Stephanie asked Celeste’s questions. Her mother shook her head, begging to skip the question, begging to be spared the humiliation of her granddaughter knowing of her foolhardy decisions. “How did you and Grandpa meet? Wasn’t he a scientist?” Celeste pressed on aggressively. Her mother answered with a mix of a nod and a shrug. Celeste sighed, gnawing on the inside of her cheek. Stephanie had provided some expounding details to Celeste and Valerie while her mother had still unabashedly talked about her father.

“Why aren’t you answering her questions?” Stephanie asked, breaking the stillness of the room.

“Because I don’t remember.” Her mother said meekly.

“Don’t remember what?”

“Any of it.” Stephanie’s breath caught, panicking. She dared to ask since when, but the absolute shame on her mother’s face implicated her. The last living recollection didn’t remember. History lost because of a vain old woman.

“Okay Grandma, I don’t need specifics, but I’m dying to know. What was it like? What was Earth like before?” Celeste had regained her composure, leaning forward.

“I don’t remember. I don’t remember.” Her mother kept repeated, pleading with Stephanie directly. Stephanie wanted to cry, having lied to her students for years.  The stories she had relied on, painfully transcribed, nothing but a foolish woman’s desire to be important, remembered.

“Mom, what is she saying?” Celeste asked in a whisper. Her mother had begun crying. Celeste rushed over, hugging her grandmother.

“She’s saying it was green and happy. Everyone got along. How Earth was alive and animated. She said it was perfect.” Stephanie said, watching as her daughter began sobbing.

After their shared crying jag, Celeste thanked her grandmother, promising to visit within the next two weeks. As they walked out, Celeste seemed to study their artificial green landscape skeptically.

“I know why she didn’t answer all my questions.” Celeste said, waiting for her mother to ask the obvious ‘why’? “It’s because Earth was so great; it’s hard to talk about leaving it, to be reminded of it. How hard it must’ve been.” Celeste continued on. “I just don’t understand how you remember nothing.” Celeste added. The comment had been without malice, but it stung enough to wake Stephanie from her stupor.

“That’s why I teach, to rekindle memories.” Celeste rolled her eyes.

“But it hasn’t helped. You should really interview Grandma in-depth. I’ll even write the questions.” Celeste said. Stephanie agreed, tilting her head back. If she squinted up into the sky, the light of their old sun could be pointed out, an ember, a remnant of brilliancy. Somewhere swirling around that sun was the husk of Earth, the sun carrying baggage around for the next few millennia. Stephanie had her own dead world, trailing her as she resolved herself to writing down the falsehoods of her mother, of a woman who had forgotten humanity. She had stranded them in the past, telling their ancestors they were no longer needed to keep progressing.

But, even Stephanie could acknowledge her mother had to be the perfect pallbearer for their history’s casket because she was nothing more than the product of their destruction. A silent, forgotten death seemed most poignant of all.

 

The End
