Fat

                                   

You’re not Greek if you’re not fat at some point in your life. Nevermind how far removed you are from the homeland, you will be fat. Usually, it’s the children that experience this, filled to the brim with butter drenched pita and pastitsio. But, you could see it in adults as well: mothers bloated from a life in the kitchen, rewarding themselves with constant parcels of food for keeping their families well-nourished and fathers with swelling stomachs from working jobs that kept them stagnant all day. I have yet to be fat, so I know my day will come.

Being fat is killing my mother. Doctors won’t call it that, of course, carefully treading the line of medical gibberish and comfort. Her cholesterol is too high, heart beating too slow, arteries clogged with all the buttery and sodium-rich foods she has been stuffing her face with since a little girl. No one told us that feta and butter could be so dangerous.

She still lives in the kitchen, scoffing at all the heart-healthy food she is supposed to be eating, her lifeboat. My father never protests as she continues to make the food we love. His favorite is lamb stuffed with garlic bulbs. She has started making the lamb too dry, overcooking it.

My father has the complexion of too-dry lamb. It’s a trait I could never really describe until that first slab of unappetizing meat disgraced my plate the first time. Greeks aren’t usually dark; my mother has a fair complexion and olive eyes. Olive in color and shape. I got my father’s darkness, a trait not welcome here. Dark was bad. Dark was the opposite of White, of European, an ethnicity that no longer belonged to us.

They speak in Greek at the dinner table. I had long ago lost my ability to comprehend them. My younger sister understands even less, pushing away the moussaka for the salad she had prepared herself. My parents both comment on my sister, calling her too thin and precocious, no wonder she hadn’t married a good Greek boy yet.

Those were the same conversations they held before I left for college. They never changed, not really.

After dinner, my father pulls both my sister and I aside. “One of you has to get married soon. For your Mana.” There is no contest: he stares directly at my sister. She rolls her eyes; she is in her second year of college.

“I’ll do it, Babas.”

“You just graduated college, no rush for you.”

“You two have had both our marriages planned since our births. No reason to hold it off any longer.” He relents, cursing in Greek under his breath.

“Thanks.” My sister and I separate wordlessly, going back to our respective rooms. I am staying with my parents as my mother proceeds to get surgeries. My salary is going towards the medical expenses and the house mortgage. I am doing what any other good Greek son would do. Loans could wait.

When I was a kid, I could jimmy my bedroom window open and lie out on the roof. It was exciting to be laying against the cool shingles at night, staring at the cloudy, endless sky. Stars were dulled, but the light they emitted was still brilliant. I would try searching for the patterns our ancestors had created to accompany the myths, but those stars were long hidden by the smog and pollution.

The roof hadn’t lost its grandeur since I had left for college. The vastness of the universe never failed to render me silent. Our problems were so small to other celestial beings, so trivial. It is chilly for a September night.

Marriage didn’t scare me. Marriage never scared Greeks. We were bred for marriage and producing a myriad of offspring. More countless than all the stars in the universe. Abraham must have been a Greek Jew.

She lives across the way, in a family so similar to mine we could be related by our experiences alone. She knows much more Greek than myself and had also gone to college to busy herself while she waited for our marriage. She likes to visit her Yiayia and Papou in Greece, often bringing back tidings from my own. A nice girl but a fat one. The girl that made most men’s dicks shrivel.

College had been a liberating experience for me. I missed it, the freedom. Although, I was startled by the campus food, shocking many people by admitting I ate lamb on a regular basis. Americans ate hamburgers and hotdogs, only seeing issue with killing sheep. Their cheeses were much duller than our traditional feta, and they lumped all Mediterranean countries into one cuisine.

But the women had been so free. I had tasted many different varieties, all appetizing in their own way. My favorites were the vocal ones with round hips to grab onto. Never fat ones though.

As I stare to the sky, I am much more overwhelmed by the possibilities of parallel universes and alien life millions of light years away than marriage. Marriage is not commitment, not for men. After I tire of her, which would happen as she grew more rotund, I would find women elsewhere, like my father before me and my Papou. The wife is to make children, to nourish me, but the mistress would be for pleasure.

I remember my father often coming home late at night, smelling of alcohol and cloaked in perfume. My mother pretended not to notice, handing him his dinner as was expected. My sister and I had seen her, the mistress once, embracing my father after mass. She had gall, hugging my father in the middle of the parking lot as my mother helped cleaning up the pews after mass.

My sister pokes her head out of the window and crawls onto the roof to join me. She hands me a joint, already burning. It is a bad habit both of us had picked up while at university, a habit I am still unwilling to give up.

“You going to propose tomorrow?”

“I guess.”

“She doesn’t even know.” I pass the joint back, feeling more relaxed. My eyes remain fixated on the stars. There is Andromeda, chained on her rock, awaiting for certain doom or to be rescued by Perseus.

“You won’t either.” My sister coughs – maybe from the weed and maybe from the suggestion. She hands me back the joint, continuing to cough.

“I’ll just get married once his mother starts dying.” I am inhaling as she spoke and snort smoke out of my nose. “For all of our myths, you would think Greeks would want love not just grandchildren.”

“They confused lust and love when the myths were created.” She sighs, following my gaze to the sky. She snatches the joint back up. “Tell me a story you’ve written.”

“To distract you?” She wants to believe I am nervous. It’s a leading question.

“No.”

“I’m just a girl writing silly love stories.” She waves her hand flippantly. I glance over at her and she rescinds her plea. She climbs back into her room and hands me several pages still fresh from the typewriter.

The story is as wonderful as her others. She weaves beauty into her earthly stories, filled with food and love. An imperfect world, one of loose men and looser women, but a gorgeous one nonetheless.

This one is different. As I continue to read, she grows fidgety.

There are autobiographical moments within the narrative, mentioning the time she ate so many cloves from a pan of baklava she threw up. A mistake only non-Greeks had made. Snippets about our father and his sexual exploits, including the scene at the church. Our mother pouring every ounce of her soul into her cooking, making us grow healthy and strong.

It delves into a love story. The food has given her strength, the protagonist, an almost direct copy of my sister. She has found love. Love for a woman.

“Do you like it?” She asks quietly, snuffing the joint out.

“You wrote an autobiography.” She shrugs.

“I wanted to try and write with my own voice for once.”

“It’s as amazing as all your other works. You made the world almost likable.” She laughs, as quietly as she can. If I listen closely enough, I can hear father snoring. She wants me to ask who she is.

“I met her at college.” She lays down beside me, hands folded underneath her head. “I like men, too, but with her, it’s different. Is it bad I believe in love?”

“There’s nothing wrong with believing in something.”

“You’re very noncommittal.” I don’t bother answering. She retreats to her room once more.

                                                            . . .

I wake on the roof as the sun rises. My eyes are blinded by its brilliance. I could understand how ancients saw a god within the sun’s rays.

“Mana, can you go on a drive with me?” My mother nods as I ask her. I had found her in the living room watching a cheesy soap opera. Breakfast had already been made for my slumbering father and sister.

“We can’t be out for too long, the Georgoulias family is coming over for dinner.” Dinner would be served at five exactly, as it was every Saturday. She would roast pork with Mrs. Georgoulias, an ailing woman with brittle bones. “Ay, where are you taking me?” She speaks in Greek, flustered. I stop in front of an engagement ring shop. She immediately begins crying.

We walk inside, and I let her decide on the ring. Silver band and amethyst center. She hugs me as I buy the ring with the little money I have left. She wraps me in her arms, suffocating me with her hug. Like being wrapped in my oldest and most worn blanket. I can’t remember her any other way than comforting.

We drive to her house. My mother is still crying, wiping her face with a handkerchief given to her by my father during their own courtship.

She didn’t know of the deals my father had made before we came to America. He ensured my sister and me, only children, would achieve the American dream. He saw only white picket fences and barbeques for us. Our papers were already in order by her family. A promise of marriage was all we needed to guarantee safe passage.

Lady Liberty had winked at me as we sailed passed her onto Ellis Island. We were some of the last immigrants through. And now, his vision of the American Dream is being realized.

I knock on her front door as if I never had before. My mother sits in the car, watching eagerly. I could spot my sister looking at me through her bedroom window. She waves, a small gesture.

Her father answers the door. I ask for his blessing. He gives it to me without hesitation. He had been initially disappointed in me and my father when we had first met. I was much darker than my pictures had shown. She was fair and plump, speaking and reading Greek with ease. I was a skinny kid with the qualities Greeks associate with Turks. However, as she grew rounder, he relented his criticisms.

She cascades down the stairs after keeping me waiting for ten minutes. She jiggles as she walks, blouse tightly stretched over her breasts. Her lack of bra is incredibly noticeable and reduces any attraction I had for her to zero. She’s simply too massive.

She whispers my name seductively, something she has been practicing since grade school. I complete the act, getting on one knee and asking for her hand in marriage. She says yes and draws me in for a wet kiss. Lips perked and plump, fully enclosing my own.

They invite our family to dinner as a celebration, but I insist they come to our home instead. My mother rushes home, exiting the car, to call the Georgoulias family to cancel plans. I kiss the top of her hand, the skin rippling from contact.

 I take her away from the ruckus. She seems calmer outside the house. Her gaze falls on the engagement ring.

“Your Mana chose the ring?”

“Yes.”

“I like it. Very Greek.” She speaks in quips.

“I’m proposing for her.” She nods, twisting her hand as the ring catches the sunlight.

“I know. I’m happy to get married. I want to leave the house.”

“I can’t leave mine yet.”

“That’s okay, it’s not this house. Thank you.” She embraces me. Her body is so similar to my mother’s but felt so much mushier. There is no tenderness in her bulk. She says thank you again, in Greek this time.

Where we were now was the spot of our first kiss, underneath the maple tree. It had been a sapling then. She had kissed me quite suddenly to see if we were good for one another. Other kids teased us about it for weeks, but she had been proud of herself. She was the first girl on the block to get a kiss.

                                                              . . .

I am back in my own house. I have long ago memorized the route between our two houses. My father eats loudly, pleased to see my mother ecstatic. He has fixed her, in his mind. The doctors knew nothing; she seems as healthy as she has ever been.

“Go tell your sister. Maybe it will inspire her to get married. She’s getting old now.” He says in between mouthfuls. Grease spills over the corners of his mouth.

“You’re engaged.” She says, not bothering to turn away from her desk. I stand in her doorway, wavering between her room and the hallway. She knows; she had watched me. Even if she hadn’t, she would’ve heard our father from the kitchen.

“It should distract them long enough for you to finish your degree.”

“I want to write something for you.” She clacks onto her typewriter before stopping and groaning. “You’re the hero no one ever writes about, protecting the women, doing his familial duty.”

“Women don’t need protection.” She huffs.

“Not always, but I do now.” She pauses and turns. Her eyes are irritated and red. She had kept her face away to cry.

Her family arrives some time later. My sister and I had been out on the roof, smoking more weed to relax before dinner. She is still on edge, biting the skin around her nails.

My mother has been slow roasting the pork since this morning. The smell wafts through the stuffy air of the house. Momentarily, I thought we were in the oven with the pork. My sister’s eyes are glazed; the same texture of the lemon and olive oil sauce smeared over the meat.

Her whole family is clustered in the living room. I am reminded of those Russian nesting dolls: her family grows increasingly larger with each older member. I start to smile, tempted to giggle. Our fathers are speaking to one another, genial. Her younger brother stares longingly at my sister.

“Agapimou.” She beckons me over to kiss me. My mother immediately begins to sob once more. She goes to the kitchen to help my mother, taking over command of cooking.

Dinner is ready soon afterwards. She has sped up the usual process. My father leads the prayer before dinner.

“And, to the couple! They really are living the American Dream.” He says after the prayer, tears welling up in his own eyes. Soon, nearly everyone is crying.

She looks at me like she loves me. “I’m going to cook so well for you.” She whispers, grasping my hand. My time to be fat is nearly upon me. My future is laid out neatly by how my parents had lived their lives.

So, I just sit there, smiling, pretending as though I am the hero, the man to be looked up to. I almost believe it myself.
