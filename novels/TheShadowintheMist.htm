The Shadow in the Mist

 

He wore sunglasses at night. Only when driving – an addendum he often forgot to mention while strolling to his car after the sun had fallen below the horizon. The questions never ceased to amaze him, the need for others to pry into his life. He had stopped answering why too long ago to remember.

The headlights from oncoming traffic made his eyes ache, the kind of ache similar to seeing an old lover and remembering every mistake made, even with his additional protection. The souls of the cars beaming out, pouring themselves past the corner and through the lens, taking root in the retina, searing themselves, their beings. Rubbing his eyes never did anything past granting momentary relief.

He went to routine check-ups, had seen every eye specialist within a 100 mile radius, greeted by varying forms of the same answer. Nothing, no root cause could be found and it wasn’t cancer. No, simply “he was getting old” or “he was getting up there.” And, he could do nothing but sit there and then agonize in pain on the drive home in full sunlight.

He felt akin to other creatures of the night, like the vampires of lore or the tangible nocturnal creatures of Earth. Darkness blanketed him, kept him warm and safe; it protected his eyes and kept his mind active.

His best work seemed to coincide with the evening, after others came home from their 9-5 jobs and ate dinner. Dinner was his breakfast. He voluntarily operated the night crews, finishing labor not completed by his daytime counterpart. To command the uneducated masses, skilled with their hands but not their minds, to argue with the young hotshots with nothing but a worthless degree and no experience to their names.

The laborers he understood; he thought of no ill towards the rural, the immigrants, doing the only work they could, honest work. It was those damn college kids he couldn’t understand. They used the computer to do their work, forgoing the pencil and protractor and ruler, the tools used since the dawn of time. They argued the computer created better schematics, clearer divides and measurement. All he saw, especially when zoomed in, were the imperfect squiggly lines. The kids couldn’t sketch anymore. They couldn’t visualize his words as he laid out a room. Upon finding a solution, he was already sketching by the time one of the kids took out a computer. Slow and inaccurate. There was a reason he was the boss.

 

He had started seeing the black dots, stars, during the daytime about a year ago. It suddenly began while looking at one of his architect’s computer rendering of the building. He had attributed the flecks to the faulty machine until they didn’t disappear. His periphery still remained cloudy. When had seen his doctor – clean bill of health, as usual – he had been warned against standing up too fast. The spots never faded, the hazy mist oozing, seeping whenever he turned.

But not in the darkness. The bliss of uniformity, more relaxing than finally settling in bed during the early twilight hours. The comfort of knowing his eyes would be shielded for a few hours, that pain did not travel to the land of unconsciousness.

 

He dreamt of such a moment as he walked to his car, sunglasses grasped tightly in his hand. The skeleton of his latest project, surrounded by the lumbering light posts casting dark shadows deep into the parking lot all loomed behind him. His car, a lone blip in the abyss. He could not be more than ten yards away from when the feeling of being hovered over, being followed. The suffocation and partiality of his vision limiting his scope. He could sense something lurking, not through echoes of footsteps or a blast of warm breath. Where was the sound? Whe –

“Hey boss man.” He fought the jolt, a hand piercing the mist. He paused, letting Reuben catch up. “Hope I didn’t scare you.” Reuben said with a half laugh tucked between his words.

“No, no.” He replied, straightening his shirt and loosening his grip on his sunglasses. “What can I help you with?”

“Me and the boys have a few ideas to run by you. The new guy showed us some sketches…” He began to ignore Reuben. The new guy, a fresh graduate from the state’s university, had already presented to him earlier, equipped printouts of all the hard work and effort he had put into them, long hours to produce a scheme doomed to failure. His eyes had ached slightly from glancing at the blueprint.

Reuben rambled on, effusively using his hands, darkness accumulating around him. He rubbed his eyes, Reuben having been relit. Pain encircled his eyes, a sharp stinging focused on the eyelids.

“We’ll talk more about this in the morning.” He interrupted, patting Reuben on the shoulder.

“You’ll actually think about it?” He nodded, breaking for his car, hand immediately massaging his temple. Opening his car, he perched his sunglasses on his nose, still tense. After deserting Reuben, the feeling of being stalked returned. A weight pressing into his back, a hold constricting his movements.

The air was stuffy in his car – did the passenger seat appear sunken? He rolled down the windows after closing the car door, ready to be smothered and covered in his bed. Shadows stretched from the tree and buildings as he drove along the road. Two strips of midnight, one physical, the other ethereal, ran parallel to one another as his car traveled in between.

There were few cars on his ride home, though his eyes did not know the difference. The same lingering degree of uncomfortableness was independent of the number of passing cars and street lights.

The air in the cabin never grew lighter either.

A sense of ease settled over him upon approaching his house. He had downgraded to a one-story house, having lived too long in a lavish three-story home for the architecture not the space. Some of him, or so he hoped, had been imprinted on that house. He regretted not vetting the interested buyer more, fearing his beauty would be reduced to nothing more than a common house.

He found himself inside his kitchen/dining room, thoughts stuck on his old home. He removed his sunglasses with care in the middle of his two seater table. Rolling his neck, he navigated his way through darkened hallways easily, no longer needing to use walls as guides.

His pain had subsided, making his collapse in bed so much more satisfying. A gladiation having defeated a worthy opponent in the arena. He would need to talk to the kid tomorrow and squash all desire to deviate from his plans, authority. He would have to be a tsar putting down a rebellion. His sleep passed by untroubled, formulating how to implement a light fixture without having the space feel more enclosed.

He opened his eyes as his alarm clock buzzed, a faint humming sound scattering nearly visible vibrations throughout his bedroom, stirring life in the enclosed space. It took him a few moments before he noticed the irritation around his eyes. He blinked involuntarily, almost to scare off the agitation. His hands trembled as he drew them from the covers, nails jagged and encrusted by dried blood.

He jolted up, mist clearing, running away before he could scream in frustration. The shadows of his nightstand and dresser became malformed as he fled down the hall to his bedroom.

Red ringlets decorated his eyes. Not the splotches from quick rubs but scratches hastily done through the night, uneven like a bird’s nest. He prodded underneath one eye cautiously, not wincing as he did so.

Movement – a figure – passed behind him. He flipped around, legs banging into the counter to see his fluttering shower curtain.

The only thought was doctor. He scuttled back to his bedroom, slamming the door behind him as an extra precaution, searching for his phone. The floorboards of the hallway creaked behind him. He spun around once more, careful to avoid his bed – knees aching, to be staring directly into his bathroom. The confusion overwhelmed him.

Cautiously, panicked but slow, measured steps were taken through his bedroom door. When he reached the opening of the bathroom his hand collided with a wall. He kept thrusting it forward, to try and get into the bathroom, only for his hand to stop on the threshold. He looked down the dim hall for the true opening and saw nothing.

Slowly, he backed into his bedroom, closing the door before looking for his phone once more. Doctor, he needed a doctor. His heart had leapt up to his throat pounding violently in his ears as he typed “optometrist” into the search bar. The results generated only one link, repeated all down the page about organ donation. He refreshed the search for the same results.

The light from his phone nipped at his eyes, the cub that would soon attack as powerfully as his father. The air began to feel stuffy from the closed door and curtains. Heat from the sun still seemed to infiltrate, raising the temperature higher.

Vainly, he searched for “Psychiatrist” only to be met with the same results of organ donation.

He covered his eyes and crawled along the floor to rest his back against his bed. To stare at his bedroom door. The door that kept opening after he closed it. It seemed to wiggle with his constant watch over it, dancing a taunting jig like some mischievous elf. Time melded together; he couldn’t bring himself to look anywhere but the door.

The room grew darker as the sun settled down. He knew he had to call someone about the job tonight, or force himself to go. He stood up shakily, legs swaying more dangerously than a fawn’s.

When he opened the door, nearly yanking it off its hinges, he faced the hallway wall. Relief spilled over, mixing with the fear, the pain, like water binding with flour. Step by step, he inched along in the darkness, back become straighter.

He mapped his plan to get to his car, pausing at the dining table, hand unable to locate his sunglasses. Reluctantly, he turned on the overhead light, startled at the stark tabletop. The light illuminated the clock’s face on the wall. He winced at his ghastly reflection and then the time. There was hesitation in his step, to leave his fortress without his shield, until the remembrance of the kid architect. He may be a silly and delusional old man at home, but he would not be usurped.

Luckily, he had not forgotten where he had placed his keys the night before and soon found himself on the road. He had ten minutes left when he remembered the ragged skin around his eyes, hanging loosely like stray strings. The headlights from other cars had caused him to temporarily forget the stinging and encroaching fog. Or that he was perspiring. He dabbed his forehead with fingers capped with dried blood, noticing the heat radiation from the car. He rolled down the front windows, unable to breath properly. The heat suffocating him, burying him.

Gasping as he parked, he flung open the car door, rolling onto the ground. His bones protested, several creaking loudly, others popping abruptly. The heat followed him, clinging to the air as he braced himself against the car. He thought about how this should have been foreseen by the doctors, his health having deteriorated in a scattering of hours.

The heat lifted, but the mist in his periphery remained. He stood up, brushing loose gravel off his clothing, listening to how they clambered against the pavement.

By the time he walked through the building husk, he felt eyes on him. He ran a hand through his thinning hair as an attempt to tame it, pulling out a sizable clump in the process. The feeling of being watched, judged, couldn’t be pushed away. His limited visual range only aggravated the feeling further. For all he knew, hundreds of eyes, of demeaning thought, could be encircling him.

He marched to the office in the back, finding the kid hunched over his desk on his phone.

“Vasily!” The kid said in shock.

“Mr. Roundtree.” He said with venom.

“We thought you weren’t going to – are you okay? Your eyes…” The kid trailed off, lips curling in disgust. He could sense someone else in the room with them and turned his head to broaden his scope. No one, just more encroaching fog.

“I’m fine.”

“You should see a doctor. Reuben and me can handle it.” The kid stood up, arms outstretched as a peacekeeping gesture.

“I’m fine.”

“Mr. Sokolov.” He glared at the youth, puffing up his chest until the kid nodded in submission, scurrying out with his head hung.

His eyes stung, one of the many peeling scabs letting blood flow down in a sticky stream. He grabbed some tissues off his desk, first dabbing then desperately pulling at the skin around his eyes. His tender skin eroded into the plush, the rock faced pounded away by the harsh elements.

The air grew heavy and hot. He tugged on his shirt collar, uncomfortable and irritated. He swung his arm down to rest against his desk, instead grabbing air and collapsing. His desk had moved five feet and no tissues were curled in his fists, just peeled off skin.

He stumbled onto his feet, tumbling out into the world, running past his employees. Voices travelled on his heels, distant cries of his name. Nothing could persuade him to return.

He started his car after running into it, hands twitching and keys jingling as his car waited to come to life. Breaths caught in his throat, caged birds trying to slip through the crack. His breathing only became more labored as the air grew muggy. The seat next to him appeared cavernous, ready to devour him. An invisible weight perched atop the itchy car interior.

The car suddenly responded, engine revving as he sped back into the darkness. He did his best to shield his eyes with his blood crusted hands – he went so far as to cover one with his palm, splitting two fingers apart to only allow the tiniest sliver of sight. His eyes began aching any time they were open. He knew he had to pull over, but the terror of not moving propelled him. The presence, the shadow in the mist, didn’t like movement.

Somehow, somehow, he managed to pull back into his driveway. He had shut off his phone during the drive, its blaring light and ringing worsening his condition. Rueben could handle work; he needed to handle himself, the delusion. They could only logically be delusions.

He walked through his front door, taking the time to lock it behind him. There seemed to be a period of ease, noticing his missing sunglasses on the table, finding all the food in his fridge unharmed. He ate some leftover pasta cold, not trusting the microwave, warily showered, and climbed into bed. He resolved he needed a good night of sleep and to drink more water. Follow the advice of the numerous doctors visited.

His sleep was troubled. He felt a burden on his chest, waking often to twist and turn to alleviate pressure. He woke to rubbing around his eyes. His dreams were a blissful black, only making his waking more so much more maddening. The word “eyes” repeated on loop when woken up, a vicious cycle of reminders.

When his alarm went off the following evening, he already felt the moisture. Perspiration – the room was hot. He peeled open his eyes, screaming at the shadow next to his bedside. A dark, shapeless figure radiating desire.

Eyes.

He jolted back, heading hitting the backboard, stars now twinkling in the mist. His cheat heaved violently, eyes bulging to try and detach themselves. The entity, the shadow, didn’t dissipate. It stood there, eyeless, expressionless.

He rolled out of his bed, scrambling to his bathroom door. Hands hit wall. He rubbed his hands arounds in increasingly violent circles, searching for where the door opening actually was. The room almost became unbearable with the heat. He felt like he would suffocate on some bizarre mix of sweat and humidity.

He clawed at his throat, collapsing on the ground, the shadow having glided over. He saw the horrors of the world encompassed in its flesh, the cries of dying children, gruesome murders. Or maybe it was the pain from the next. His whole body seemed to pulsate, set ablaze with various ailments. Muscles cramped, heart stopping.

When the pain subsided, when he stopped withering in pain, he crawled into the easily accessible open door to the hallway. He felt it follow, the kite to his kite string. Into the kitchen, pulling himself up using the table.

He grasped the sunglasses laying on the surface with both hands, facing it. It would not best him. He opened his sunglasses up, staring at them before taking the bit that hooked onto his ear and ramming it into his right eye. He almost fainted from the pain, desperately tearing away at veins and muscles until the eye fell out with a satisfying pop. It bounced once on the floor, away from him. As the blood continued to waterfall from the cavity, he busied himself with the other.

 

…

 

The paramedics had been kind and understanding when they arrived. He had supposedly called 911. They had found him passed out on the floor, broken and dripping sunglasses a few feet away from him. His eyes weren’t recovered – they weren’t found.

The doctor had told him he had a tumor in his eye socket that had caused all the pain, maybe even the origin of his delusions. The doctor had corrected it with no issues, but did recommend voluntary commitment. He had happily obliged, wanting to extend the transition period between sight and its absence.

He had sick leave saved up for such occasions, trying to formulate a response to quitting. His savings were enough to live off of and he considered applying for disability.

He spent his days in bliss, darkness, nothingness, coddling him like never before. The absolute bliss. He knew he would miss being able to appreciate architecture, his children, but the irritation-free existence proved too intoxicating.

But, but, when he dreamed, he would still see. The doctors told him this was common and not to worry. The worry didn’t stem from the sight but what he saw.

Every night, he stood at the foot of a little girl’s bed, aroused. He’d focus on her ears, how soft and shapely and capable they were. He wanted to stroke them, to tell her of his desire, but he could not do so. He noted the hearing aids on her nightstand – she didn’t need ears, sounds. Her perfect world was one of silence – look how peaceful she slept. When the sun would begin to rise, he would turn to leave. He would pause in front of the mirror, seeing his eyes bloodshot and malformed and would scream until he woke up. For night had betrayed him, made him unnatural, for he had become the shadow.
